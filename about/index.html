What This Is

This is a collection of materials I've built to teach others, or sometimes just myself. I'm a visual learner and I tend to ask endless questions before accepting a proof, a fact, or a method. Why does this work? What if this assumption fails? Why should I believe this step follows from the last?

It's tedious. But doing that work means I remember things and can actually manipulate the concepts afterwards, rather than just following procedures I don't understand.

Most of these materials started as notes for myself or explanations for peers. I thought I'd collect them here into something more coherent. And since visuals are how I learn best, I wanted to make the presentation actually support that kind of learning rather than fight against it.

I also believe formalism matters. Proofs matter. The mathematics beyond introductory statistics matters. So this website is built to walk in that direction over time. That's why it takes the form of series with multiple episodes, building from intuition toward rigour.

If there's one principle throughout: these materials treat you as someone who asks questions rather than someone who accepts procedures.

Who I Am

I'm Sacha Bechara. Final-year Politics and International Relations student at University College London.

I hated mathematics throughout secondary school. Avoided it entirely. I had decided I wasn't a quantitative person and that was that.

Then in my first year of university I had to take a module covering introductory inferential and descriptive statistics, probability, and epistemology in science. I remember sitting in a lecture being told there's a line of best fit. It minimizes the errors. And thinking: why this line? Where does it come from? Is it magic? Does it just appear out of a computer?

That kind of question turned out to be the thing that made statistics make sense to me. I stopped telling myself I was simply bad at maths and started treating formulas the way I'd been trained to treat normative arguments in political theory. I'd imagine someone trying to convince me that a formula was true, and I'd be the skeptic, asking a thousand questions before accepting anything. Why this step? What justifies that move? What breaks if you change this?

It was painful at first. Slow. I'd spend hours on things other people seemed to accept in minutes. But over time I learned to sit with confusion until I found answers that actually convinced me. And once something convinced me, I didn't forget it.

Now most of my research is quantitative. I study judicial behavior in climate litigation, child abuse reporting patterns, spatial distributions in crime data. I build databases, write R packages, run simulations. I went from avoiding maths to organising my work around it.

These materials come from that. They're not written by an expert. They're written by someone who remembers not understanding, who had to find their own way in, and who thinks "just memorize it" is never an acceptable answer to "I don't get it."
