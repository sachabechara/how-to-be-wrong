Causal Inference

From Intuition to Identification

Does playing hard to get actually work? Do morning routines cause success? Does therapy help, or do people who seek therapy recover on their own?

These questions feel answerable. You see patterns, you draw conclusions. But patterns deceive. The confident conclusions we draw from observation often collapse under scrutiny.

This series builds the logic of causal thinking from scratch. No prerequisites beyond curiosity. We start with intuition — why the obvious approach fails — and slowly assemble the machinery that lets us make defensible causal claims.

By the end, you'll have both the geometric intuition and the formal apparatus to reason rigorously about cause and effect.

On Formalism

This series does not shy away from mathematics. You will encounter formal notation, derivations, and proofs — but always motivated by intuition first.

Variance & Covariance Properties

  • Var(X + c) = Var(X)
  • Var(cX) = c²Var(X)
  • Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y) + 2Cov(X,Y)
  • Cov(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]
  • Cov(aX, bY) = ab·Cov(X,Y)
  • Law of total variance: Var(Y) = E[Var(Y|X)] + Var(E[Y|X])

Named Theorems & Inequalities

  • König-Huygens theorem: Var(X) = E[X²] - E[X]²
  • Cauchy-Schwarz inequality: |Cov(X,Y)| ≤ SD(X)·SD(Y)
  • Chebyshev's inequality: probability bounds from variance
  • Gauss-Markov theorem: OLS as best linear unbiased estimator
  • Cramér-Rao lower bound: minimum variance of unbiased estimators
  • Central Limit Theorem: asymptotic normality of sample means

Estimator Derivations

  • Unbiasedness of the sample mean
  • Derivation of Var(X̄) = σ²/n
  • Unbiasedness of difference-in-means under randomization
  • Variance of the ATE estimator
  • Neyman's repeated sampling framework

The goal is not mathematical sophistication for its own sake, but a deep understanding of why the machinery works — and when it breaks.

Prerequisites

Formal prerequisites: none. Comfort with high school algebra helps but isn't required.

Everything is built from scratch. If you've never seen a summation sign or a square root, you'll learn what they mean here. If you have a statistics background, you may find fresh perspectives on familiar concepts.
